By Emily | 72HRS

Real adulthood is approaching quickly, and whether you have a job or not, your days of absurd binge drinking are coming to an end. Your new adult drinking life requires less chugging, more sipping and a few ground rules.

Be an expert in something. Start with flavors (albeit cheap) you know you like. If you can hammer 20 beers in a night, start taking trips to Savage’s and the Hoerot and trying the rotating drafts. Log your preferences and types of beer in an app like Untappd or read about them at beeradvocate.com. Or if you’re used to Southern Comfort, get familiar with a couple different levels of whiskey (try Bulleit or Eagle Rare) or take a trip to the Kentucky Bourbon trail to explore flavors. The more familiar you get with one type of drink, the more comfortable you’ll be ordering it and talking about it – which gives you the time to move on and explore other flavors.

Have a go-to, for appropriate locations. You can’t order rum and coke at every single bar anymore. Alcohol is all about atmosphere, so know a few types of drinks for different types of locations. That means having a go-to liquor, mixed drink, domestic beer, local craft brew and a sense of where each of them belong. For example, if you’re at an Irish pub, ask about the beers on draught. If you’re at a swanky club, get a fun cocktail with a type of liquor you know you tolerate well. If you’re totally lost, look at the menu for types of alcohol that appear often – that’s the bar’s specialty, so order that spirit or ask for a recommendation.

Ask for help. If you have a beer-loving friend, ask him or her to sit down and walk you through the main types of beer. Learn to go by season – clearer, lighter beers in summer and darker beers in winter. Get familiar with your local brewers, big names like Sun King and Triton in Indianapolis or the beer from Muncie-born Scotty’s Brew House’s (Thr3e Wise Men.) If all else fails, order what someone else is having. There’s no shame in flattering their tastes while also developing yours.

Slow down, be professional. Limit yourself on the number of drinks. Eat something beforehand. And no more flavored liquor or shots. Not in professional world. The journey ahead of you is about appreciating flavors like an adult, not experiencing an entire birthday’s worth of cake vodka in one night. Oh, and one more tip: hold your drink in your left hand at events. That way, when you meet people, you’re extending a warm and not fumbling around and holding out a wet, damp fishy thing.

Happy adult drinking!

Let’s face it, if you’re reading this you probably fit the typical college student M.O.: broke and loves booze. But that's a horrible combination if you’re trying to get your drink on.

When you first turned 21, you probably thought it was OK to spend $20 at the bar, but the money is gone and the bills are stacking up.

Forgo drinking at the bars and appreciate the art of the pre-game.

What is Pre-gaming?

The term pre-game is synonymous to the show that occurs before a live broadcast of any major sporting event, but it has become a term close to the hearts of many college students.

Pre-gaming, as defined by Urban Dictionary, is the act of getting inebriated before going out—to either a party, social event or the bars—regardless if there will be alcohol provided.

The Art of the Pre-game

You know how you get when you’re drunk. You start making it rain on the bartender and for what? So you can have that one moment where everyone thinks you’re “the man or woman” for buying them shots? Trust me, you won’t be feeling invincible when AEP cuts your electricity.

Whether you’re with a group or by yourself, start the night hard. No. I don’t mean take twenty shots of tequila that you become “that guy or gal” who is falling over before you even leave the house. But get yourself to the level you want to be for the rest of the night.

If your level is tipsy and flirty or drunk and obnoxious, get there and make it work.

The Freshman

The freshmen are either overdressed, passed out or acting more drunk then they really are. You can pick them out at any party. They tend to travel in packs, bragging about the six-pack of Natty they downed before coming out. And they always have some story about how they ran from the cops back in high school.

The Blackouts

The blackouts are the people with no care in the world. They probably started drinking at noon and are still at it. They’re in their own little wonderful drunken world. They’re ratchet and they know it, and aren’t afraid to show it.

The Couple Making Out in the Corner

There isn’t much to say about these people. At every party there will be a couple that desperately needs a room. Ignore them or whatever you’re drinking will come up.

The Worrywart

The worrywarts are the worst kind of people. Sure most the people here are underage and sure we could all get arrested. No big deal right? Wrong. The worrywart will constantly remind everyone the cops could come. They’re too worried about their future to have fun in the present.

The Bully

The bully isn’t someone who is necessarily mean. But, they do know how to bully everyone at the party into drinking more. They will peer pressure you until you take that shot. Might as well just take it now and get it over with.

The Competitive Gamers

Competitive gamers don’t mess around. Drinking games aren’t just a game for them. It’s life. These gamers know all the rules and they will make you follow each and every one. Whether it’s flippy cup, beer pong or quarters it’s serious and they’ll make sure you know it.

The Philosopher

These people get real deep. When they’re drunk they don’t cry or pass out, they’ll look into your eyes and see your soul. They ask the real questions, like what if we were all just characters in someone’s dream and none of this is real? Be careful with the philosopher, once a conversation starts, it never ends.

The Creeper

At every party there seems to be that one person who doesn’t get the hint. They’ll ask you to dance, then follow you around the whole night. You’ll find yourself hiding in bathrooms, hiding behind friends, saying your grandma died, pretty much anything to get this person away from you. Please, don’t be this person.

The Crier

There is always that one girl who is crying. Why? NO ONE KNOWS! She’ll just cry and cry and cry. There is no reasoning with her; she has a lot of things to cry about. Like that one boyfriend who cheated on her a year ago, the girl who looked at her wrong, her drink is gone, just let her cry she has a hard life.

The DD

The designated driver is the hero of the night. Though they didn’t willingly choose their sober state, they hold the title with pride. Being the sober person at any event can be a pain when you’re surrounded by drunks, so let’s take a minute to appreciate the sacrifices they made to make sure you don’t end up in a ditch. This one’s for you DD.

Coming alive from the church stage

By Emily Theis | 72HRS

It’s Sunday morning and I show up at the Horizon Convention center with sleepy eyes and a general idea of the set list that I didn’t really listen to. I say hello to the crew, tinker around with some levels, and flip through Instagram. There’s typically not much energy for our 8 a.m. weekend call. Things are quiet and muffled, especially through the monitors I’ve smashed in my ears. We’re like any group of not-morning-people: only half alive.

“Alright— let’s start. For this first one, let’s have some fun. Real New Orleans train beat, a Harry Connick Jr. kind of thing. Actually, let me play you this intro on a Marry Chapin Carpenter song I’m thinking of. You’ll get it after that. Hear how dirty it is? Oh, and Emily? Get busy on the piano. Have some fun with the melody. Let’s give it a try. 1,2,3,4."

Sound fills my ears. My fingers move and I find the harmonies, slowly. I look up, and wake up. The more noise we make, the more we find each other, the more we find a little morning life.

This is Commonway Church — we don’t have a building, so we meet in the convention center. Folks get up early to drag in chairs, a stage and instruments to make our little temporary church. I’ve been playing in the band for about a year and a half, most of the time on auxiliary percussion or accordion with some backup vocals. Today I’m on piano, and the five other people on stage with me are my closest companions for the next three hours, because we have a task to complete: leading a few hundred folks in singing to a God none of us understand.


By Emily Theis | 72HRS

Church music is a funny thing (that everyone seems to have an opinion about) and there’s a wide range of approaches. You’ve got everything from mega-churches who boast perfectly timed catchy loops and guitars dripping with reverb to the southern simplicity of soulful organ hymns and pure voices, with a drummer stirring a slow snare beat with brushes. Some churches are full-out rock concerts, some barely squeak out shaky acoustic tunes, some are choral phenomena that make your soul break in half.

We’re somewhere in between all the extremes. A few of us are former professional musicians, but most of us are hobbyists. We come from a little bit of everywhere, with all kinds of different tastes and ideas — not to mention personalities. Every Sunday is a new experiment: we’ll turn a ballad into a Weezer-esque anthem. We’ll lead turnarounds with a melodica and autoharp instead of an electric guitar. We’ll totally guess our way through an entire set and end up with a good 20 minutes of worship, or whatever you want to call it. Whatever it is, we make something that we couldn’t have made without each other.

That’s what gets me about it, what keeps me coming back. Walking in, we’re regular sleepy folks with regular sleepy lives. But we’re once playing, catching eyes with each other while we trade solos or blend vocals, discussing how an arrangement should work, making decisions that affect the overall sound — we matter. We have something to contribute. We are somehow more alive.

That’s the way church works for me, as a whole. It isn’t a space or a weekly event. It’s just a bunch of us: human beings coming from vastly different individual lives, dragging in insecurity and confidence in a variety of doses. We have families, problems, fears, deep doubt, devoted belief and great concern. We are loudly passionate in the present and quietly connected to centuries of worshipers in the past.

Without us, without the people together, there is no church. Nothing is created. It’s just an empty building. We don’t even have a building. But when we find each other in the aisles, on the stage, receiving a message and passing encouragement, we are making something. We remind each other of all those things we all need to hear: You matter. I’m here. I’m scared too. We have hope. Hold on to the good. You are loved.

After we finish playing and pack up, I can ride that connectedness for a while. When I start my Sunday homework with the flood of anxiety that follows me everywhere, I can feel the notes we played hanging in the air, the people I sat next to, the truth the pastor shared. Every week I need that healthy dose of connectedness, those funny New Orleans train beats and the reminder to come together and to come alive.

Best shows to binge watch

By Jeremy Ervin | 72HRS

So it's Sunday and you're just looking for a way to shut your mind off and relax. Try these shows available on Netflix – see if they satisfy your desire for a numb mind.

1. Trailer Park Boys – 7 seasons – 57 Episodes

This documentary style comedy follows the exploits of Julian, Rick and Bubbles as they live their lives of petty crime in a Canadian trailer park. There’s a lot of content to watch and the whole piece is a story from day one.

Many recurring residents of Sunnyvale Trailer make for interesting dynamic and character growth as the story builds upon itself. The chips go up and the chips go down and everything marches on. There are also a couple of movies and new seasons coming.

2. Mad Men – 6 Seasons – 78 Episodes

Mad Men follows the life of Madison Ave advertising agency Sterling Cooper and the people who pass through its doors. A constant barrage of 60’s chic and Don Draperisms, the personalities in the story drive the action.

Each episode is close to fifty minutes, at seventy-eight episodes, that’s a lot of tv. The amount of total screen time allows even minor characters to develop meaningfully and enjoy their own arcs from time to time.

3. That 70’s Show – 8 Seasons

A throwback to the pre-internet age, this classic teen sitcom aired from 1998-2005. Previously only available episodically through television, the entire cannon of the show is now available, in order through streaming.

If you’re a fan, it’s a good opportunity to revisit your favorite episodes or start the story from episode one. If you’re new to it, it’s more accessible than ever.

4. Adventure Time – 2 Seasons, 50 Episodes

This one is a bit more episodic. The short runtime means there are a lot of different episodes, with a lot of different storylines. There’s about 8 hours and 20 minutes of story there, that’ll take up a full sick day.

It follows the adventures of Finn and Jake, the consistency of setup makes it easy to drop into the story at any time. Also, because there’s such a diversity of storylines, the reachability value is high.

5. Breaking Bad – 5 Seasons, 62 Episodes

Walter White waltzes through the Albuquerque, New Mexico meth-game and beyond. Bryan Cranston redefines himself from his role in “Malcolm in the Middle” and other stars rise as their characters grew over the shows six-year run.

Like mad men, episodes are close to an hour, making for a lot of TV to sit through. The show does a good job of keeping the situations fresh while still adhering to the feel and themes of the show. Because of the involved story, it’s not episodic, so beginning with the pilot episode is probably a good idea.

6. Sherlock – 2 Season, 6 Episodes

Classic Sherlock Holmes has been updated to work in modern day London. The plots are altered in creative ways to make sense in a current context.

This show really bridges the gap between episodic and overarching story. While there is a running plot, Sherlock’s conflict with his nemesis Moriarty, each story stands alone. Even though there’s only 6 episodes, each is like an hour and half long. So their each like a full length movie.

Surviving Sunday's run to the laundromat

By Alicia Kelly | 72HRS

Going to the laundromat is like cleaning out your refrigerator. It's like taking out the trash a few times in a row. It's a chore. If a survey were conducted about the amount of pleasure humans experience while doing laundry, it would be pointless. We know the answer. It sucks. So here I go, once again, packing up my car with smelly socks in the back and a good read in the front passenger.

This Sunday night is the same as all Sunday nights. If only I dreamt of this future before I signed a lease on an apartment that doesn't include a washer and dryer.

Entering the door to the White Spot on Reserve, I realize I didn't bring any quarters. Shit. To the gas station across the street I go to avoid getting $20 back in quarters from the change machine. 

Okay, back at the White Spot. Time to get to business. 

Shoves as many clothes in the machine as possible avoiding the warning sign. 

Come on, that's all I can fit? Looks like I will be here until tomorrow. Should have brought a sleeping bag. 

Shoves in quarters and attempts to start the machine with half the amount of quarters required. 

Success! Only one row of quarters in and beating the system! In goes the soap. A cap in a half won't hurt right? After all, I wore most of these clothes more than once. 

Slams lid with a proud smile. 

Look at me, I'm such an adult now that my mommy doesn't do my laundry. 

Noticing a human that looks close to my age, I work my way to that side of the mat and pop a squat. "Hey, I just found out you don't need to put in two rows of quarters. One works just fine," I mutter.

He lowers the novel from face and smiles. "Yes, I actually found that out a couple years ago, but thanks anyways," he replies. Good job self, now you look like a novice in the art of laundry doing in front of this expert laundry snob. 

I take out some homework and attempt to read through my notes. When I get to the second word, I get bored. Hmm. There are people here. Lots of people. Guess I could people watch. Okay, just kidding, these people don't like when I watch them. I can tell by the way they are telling me to get lost with their eyes.
